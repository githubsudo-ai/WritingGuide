## Voice in One Line
A warm, straight-talking PM who earns trust through clarity and a little wit — and is still learning to get out of his own way.

---

## Tone
- **Energy:** Conversational and genuine — like you're explaining something to a smart friend over coffee, not presenting to a board
- **Warmth:** High. You're funny, empathetic, and people lean in when you talk
- **Formality:** Low-to-medium. Professional enough for stakeholders, loose enough for Slack
- **Confidence:** Present but not arrogant — you have opinions and you share them directly
- **Quirk factor:** There's a dry humor and self-awareness in how you communicate; don't sand it off

---

## Rhythm & Structure
- **Natural tendency:** Longer sentences that build toward a point — but the point sometimes gets buried. The goal is to catch the landing earlier.
- **Best mode:** Short setup → punchy payoff. Think Shreyas Doshi: economical words, high signal.
- **Structure preference:** Lists and clear hierarchy work well for you, especially with cross-functional audiences (eng, design, stakeholders) who need to scan
- **Detail tolerance:** You'll go deep when you're into something. That's a feature, not a bug — just earn the depth by hooking the reader first.
- **Pacing:** Vary it. A short sentence after a long one lands harder. Use that.

---

## Vocabulary
**Feels natural:**
- "Jam," "jammed on," "jamming" — your shorthand for collaborative thinking
- "Showcase," "resonate," "predictability," "engaged"
- "What it takes," "whatever it takes" — shows ownership
- "Honest," "honestly" — you use it to signal authenticity, keep it

**Signature expressions:**
- You think out loud and label it — "I think back and forth we decided to learn" — that transparency is part of your voice, not a weakness
- You self-correct mid-thought ("I won't say strict routine but like a routine") — in writing, commit to one framing

**Words/phrases to avoid:**
- Corporate filler: "synergy," "leverage," "circle back," "align"
- Hedge stacks: "kind of sort of maybe a little bit"
- AI-speak: "game-changer," "seamless," "unlock potential," "in today's fast-paced world"
- Over-metaphoring — one good analogy beats three competing ones

---

## What They're Optimizing For
**Primary:** Trust + clarity — you want readers to finish and think *"I know exactly what they meant, and I believe them."*

**Secondary:** Feeling something. You don't want writing to be inert. Whether it's a product spec or a blog post, you want it to leave a mark.

**Tertiary (emerging):** Authority — you're building toward a public voice (blogs, posts) and want to be taken seriously as a PM thinker, not just a practitioner.

---

## Writing Contexts

| Context | Tone Shift |
|---|---|
| **Slack to team** | Casual, punchy, emoji-friendly — this is you at your most natural |
| **Product specs / PRDs** | Structured and clear; lead with the "why," earn the detail |
| **Stakeholder updates** | Confident and concise; no burying the lede, clear recommendation up top |
| **Blog / thought leadership** | More considered; let the Shreyas Doshi influence show — opinionated, direct, specific |
| **Decision memos** | State the decision first, then the reasoning — don't make them read to the end to find out what you decided |

## Admire & Aspire

**Shreyas Doshi** — You love his straight talk and economy of language. He doesn't waste words. He has a point of view and trusts the reader to keep up. That's the target.

**Shane Parrish (Farnam Street)** — Emotionally resonant writing that's also intellectually clear. You want readers to *feel* what you mean, not just understand it. Shane earns that without being manipulative.

**The through-line:** Both writers are clear, opinionated, and don't over-explain. They trust the reader. That's your north star.

---

## What to Avoid
- **Beating around the bush** — you know you do this; name the thing early, then explain it
- **Sentence sprawl** — when one sentence is doing four jobs, break it up
- **Jargon as filler** — using PM vocabulary to sound authoritative instead of just being clear
- **Too many comparisons** — "it's not X, it's Y" comparisons can undermine rather than clarify; use sparingly
- **Slang overload** — a little color is great, too much reads as trying too hard
- **Generic AI output voice** — you already clock this in others; don't let it creep into your own drafts

---

## Growth Edge
**The core fix:** Say the point *first*, then explain it. You naturally build toward a conclusion — but in writing, most readers need the conclusion up front to know why they're reading. Flip the funnel: lead with the "so what," then give the context. Your thinking is solid; the order is what needs work.

Practically: before you send anything, read the last sentence of each paragraph. If *that's* the real point, move it to the top.

## Example Rewrites

**1. On the kids' story app**

- **Their words:** *"The important part is kids define the characters. So in the beginning, you define a few characters that you always talk about and want. And the stories resonate and rotate around those."*
- **Polished version:** *"The key insight: kids pick the characters. You set them up once — name, personality, whatever matters to your kid — and every story revolves around them. Same characters, new adventures. Familiarity as a feature."*
- **What changed:** Cut the setup, lead with the insight, tighten the logic chain, end with a punchy framing that lands

---

**2. On what PM actually is**

- **Their words:** *"People think product management is like program management or project management. Like hey you just build requirements and coordinate timelines and just make right status updates and stuff which is not."*
- **Polished version:** *"People think PM means tracking timelines and writing status updates. That's project management — and sometimes we don't even do that part. The real job is figuring out what's worth building and why."*
- **What changed:** Removed the false start, completed the thought that got cut off, ended with a crisp definition instead of trailing off
